\begin{abstract}
Managing cryogenic propellants in microgravity poses significant challenges, particularly safe venting without liquid entrainment. During tank depressurization, bulk boiling and wall boundary-layer phenomena drive a transient rise of the liquid--vapor interface that can carry liquid into the vent line, causing propellant loss and vent-system damage.

This study modernizes the legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model, originally developed following the Saturn AS-203 flight experiment. The original Fortran and Visual Basic for Applications (VBA) implementation has been rewritten as a parallelized Python framework integrated with CoolProp to provide high-accuracy real-fluid thermodynamic properties for multiple cryogens.

Parametric sweeps were performed across initial fill levels, vent flow rates, tank aspect ratios, and microgravity regimes from $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}\,$g to evaluate three candidate test platforms: the NASA Glenn Research Center (GRC) 2.2-second drop tower, the NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, and parabolic flight campaigns. The 5-second zero-gravity facility offers the best balance of microgravity quality and test duration to capture the maximum measurable liquid level rise (LLR). Optimal initial fill fractions are identified to prevent lid impingement.

Predictive correlations for pressure-driven liquid level rise in microgravity remain anchored to a single 1966 in-orbit data set, leaving a critical gap for the next generation of cryogenic propellant systems. The computational framework and recommended test matrix presented here lay the foundation for a sub-meter-scale ground experiment that will deliver the high-quality, flight-relevant liquid--vapor interface data the community currently lacks, providing the empirical basis needed to advance correlations for cryogenic fluid management.
\end{abstract}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Cryogenic Fluid Management, Microgravity, Zero-Gravity Venting, Liquid Level Rise, Computational Fluid Dynamics, LIQLEV, Drop Tower Testing.

\section{Introduction}
As space exploration architectures increasingly depend on cryogenic propellants---liquid hydrogen (LH$_2$), liquid oxygen (LO$_x$), and liquid methane (LCH$_4$)---robust cryogenic fluid management (CFM) technologies are essential. In microgravity, diminished buoyancy allows surface tension to dominate fluid behavior, distorting the liquid--vapor interface.

When a cryogenic tank is vented to relieve pressure from parasitic heat leak, the rapid depressurization triggers thermodynamic flashing and bulk boiling. Vapor generated in the superheated liquid bulk and along the wetted tank walls forms bubbles that displace surrounding liquid, raising the liquid--vapor interface. If the resulting two-phase mixture reaches the vent port, liquid entrainment causes propellant loss and damage to the venting infrastructure.

Predicting this transient liquid level rise (LLR) is critical for safe and efficient vent-system design. Early experimental investigations by Labus et al.~\cite{Labus1972, Labus1974} at the NASA Lewis Research Center 5-second zero-gravity facility (now NASA Glenn Research Center) provided foundational insights into refrigerant venting under reduced gravity. The Saturn S-IVB AS-203 flight experiment~\cite{AS203_1970} subsequently produced the most comprehensive in-orbit data on LH$_2$ behavior during venting and motivated the development of the LIQLEV analytical boundary-layer model.

This paper presents the modernization of the LIQLEV model and its application to the Fiscal Year 2025 (FY25) Drop Tower LLR Study. The objectives are to (i)~translate the legacy code to Python, (ii)~broaden its thermodynamic modeling capability across cryogens, and (iii)~perform parametric sweeps over tank geometry, vent rate, fill level, and gravity level. These analyses identify experimental conditions that capture a measurable peak interface height within the limited test durations of three reduced-gravity facilities: the NASA Glenn Research Center (GRC) 2.2-second drop tower, the GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, and parabolic flight campaigns.

\subsection{Need and Significance}
\label{sec:need_significance}

Long-duration human and robotic missions to the Moon and Mars rely on in-space cryogenic propellants for descent stages, ascent stages, transfer vehicles, and propellant depots. NASA's Moon-to-Mars architecture, the Human Landing System (HLS), and proposed nuclear thermal and electric propulsion stages all require storing and managing LH$_2$, LO$_x$, and LCH$_4$ for weeks to months in microgravity~\cite{Meyer2019, Hartwig2015}. Reliable vent operations---relieving ullage pressure without expelling liquid---are enabling capabilities for each mission class.

The same depressurization that relieves ullage pressure also induces bulk flashing and wall-driven boundary-layer boiling, and the resulting two-phase swelling can drive the liquid--vapor interface into the vent line. The consequence is liquid carryover, propellant residuals, and (in the worst case) vent-system damage~\cite{Hastings2003, Hartwig2015}. Mission-level analyses of in-space depots place propellant residuals and boil-off losses at several percent per month under poorly characterized vent conditions~\cite{Plachta2014, Meyer2019}, directly eroding payload capacity and $\Delta v$ margin.

Despite the maturity of the underlying physics, predictive correlations for transient LLR under microgravity venting remain anchored to a single in-orbit data set: the Saturn S-IVB AS-203 LH$_2$ experiment of 1966~\cite{AS203_1970}. Subsequent ground campaigns---including the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization test~\cite{tani2021pressure} and the ISS-based Zero Boil-Off Tank (ZBOT) experiments~\cite{Kassemi2018}---have advanced the understanding of self-pressurization and active pressure control, yet none resolve the boundary-layer-driven LLR transient at flight-relevant gravity levels in tanks of sub-meter scale. The gap is acute for HLS-class vehicles, where small ullage margins and aggressive vent duty cycles place LLR on the critical path for vent-system sizing~\cite{NASA80NSSC24K1219}.

This paper addresses the gap in three steps. First, the legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model is reimplemented as a parallelized, CoolProp-integrated Python framework with a Numba just-in-time (JIT) compiled solver that delivers $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the prior pure-Python implementation, enabling thousand-case parametric sweeps in minutes rather than hours. Second, the modernized model is verified against the JAXA large-scale LH$_2$ data and cross-validated with three-dimensional volume-of-fluid (VOF) computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Third, the verified framework is used to down-select a recommended experimental test matrix for the NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, providing pre-test predictions and instrumentation requirements for a planned drop-tower campaign that will yield the first sub-meter-scale, flight-relevant LLR data set for CFM correlation development.

\section{Background and Literature Review}

\subsection{Early Experimental Investigations}
Foundational zero-gravity venting studies were conducted by Labus et al.~\cite{Labus1972, Labus1974} at the NASA Lewis Research Center 5-second zero-gravity facility. The tests examined cylindrical acrylic containers partially filled with initially saturated Refrigerant 11 (R11), octofluorocyclobutane (RC318), and n-butane (R600). The test liquids exhibited near-zero contact angles on the acrylic walls, producing hemispherical liquid--vapor interfaces under zero gravity.

Representative test sequences reveal a strong dependence of boiling behavior on vent rate. At a high vent rate ($\sim$1.0 ullage volume per second using RC318), significant bulk boiling appeared shortly after venting began: vapor generation accelerated near the liquid--vapor interface, drove the interface upward toward the vent, and produced visible bubbles within the bulk liquid (Figure~\ref{fig:labus_1p0}).

At a lower rate of 0.5 ullage volumes per second with the same fluid, bulk boiling did not occur. Vaporization was confined almost entirely to the free surface, the interface remained nearly stationary, and no bulk-liquid bubbles formed (Figure~\ref{fig:labus_0p5}).

These observations established that interfacial evaporation and bulk boiling both contribute to the venting response, with the transition between the two regimes governed primarily by vent rate. Interfacial mass transfer was shown to control the ullage pressure response, particularly at low vent rates. Comparison against a conduction-based model assuming a flat interface further demonstrated that accurate pressure-decay predictions require explicit treatment of mass transfer at the interface.

\subsection{The Saturn S-IVB AS-203 Flight Experiment}
The most comprehensive in-orbit data on cryogenic liquid behavior during venting were obtained during the Saturn S-IVB AS-203 mission, launched on 5 July 1966~\cite{AS203_1970}. The experiment executed three rapid LH$_2$-tank depressurization sequences using both the Continuous Vent System (CVS) and the Non-Propulsive Vent (NPV) system. The pressure reductions (several psi each) occurred while the vehicle was in a low-gravity coast environment of $\sim 10^{-4}$ to $10^{-5}\,$g.

The S-IVB LH$_2$ tank was instrumented with temperature diodes at multiple axial stations and dedicated liquid--vapor point sensors. Of particular importance was sensor N052, co-located with temperature diode C0347 just above the nominal interface (approximately 43\,\% fill at station 438). N052 was a variable-resistance device similar to those used on the Centaur vehicle and was the only liquid--vapor sensor that operated reliably; its wetting and drying indications agreed closely with the temperature response of the co-located diode. The remaining liquid--vapor sensors exhibited anomalous behavior---apparently trapping liquid once wetted and producing persistent erroneous wet indications.

During the second and third depressurization events, N052---initially residing in the ullage---registered a transition to liquid contact, providing direct evidence of substantial upward displacement of the interface. Temperature diodes near the original interface revealed superheated regions followed by rapid cooling at boiling onset. Quantitative analysis showed that the observed LLR substantially exceeded the rise attributable to simple flashing or volumetric expansion, scaling strongly with both the magnitude and rate of pressure reduction (Figure~\ref{fig:as203_llr_vs_dp}).

These low-gravity venting observations established that vapor generation in the wall thermal boundary layer, in combination with bulk flashing, produces significant bulk-liquid displacement. The AS-203 data therefore became the definitive validation benchmark and motivated the original LIQLEV boundary-layer model, which explicitly accounts for wall-driven boil-off and its contribution to interface rise.

\subsection{Development of the Original LIQLEV Model}
Following the AS-203 flight, the LIQLEV (Liquid Level) boundary-layer model was developed in Fortran to predict the contribution of wall boundary-layer boil-off to bulk-liquid displacement. The model, fully documented in Appendix B of NASA CR-109847, treats vapor generation within a thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted sidewall and its effect on the upward motion of the liquid--vapor interface.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/saturn_boundary_layer_model.png}
\caption{Bubble boundary-layer model developed for the AS-203 liquid hydrogen tank \cite{AS203_1970}. The model treats vapor generation within a thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted sidewall, with bubbles contributing to bulk liquid displacement.}
\label{fig:liqlev_boundary_layer}
\end{figure}

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_boundary_layer}, the model treats the sidewall as a surface on which a growing vapor film forms; bubbles nucleate, grow, and detach within the film, displacing liquid and raising the interface. The governing physics are expressed via unsteady mass and energy balances on differential elements of the boundary layer (Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_mass_balance}), yielding the instantaneous boundary-layer vapor volume $V_{\rm BL}(t)$ and the corresponding interface velocity
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt},
\]
where $A_c$ is the tank cross-sectional area. Parametric studies examined the sensitivity of LLR to vent rate, tank diameter, gravity level, and bubble spacing.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/saturn_mass_balance.png}
\caption{Mass balance for a differential element of the unsteady thermal boundary layer in the original LIQLEV model \cite{AS203_1970}}
\label{fig:liqlev_mass_balance}
\end{figure}

More recently, Moran~\cite{MoranVBA} adapted the original Fortran code into an Excel VBA environment in support of the HLS program. This adaptation required meticulous line-by-line debugging of the legacy source from the poorly scanned NASA CR-109847 report. Moran's implementation replicated the AS-203 flight conditions, replaced outdated hydrogen property curve fits with calls to the CoolProp library, and introduced a dynamic ratio $\epsilon$ representing the proportion of boil-off generated in the wall boundary layer relative to the free surface. The ratio was made dependent on the wetted-wall and free-surface areas.

While functional, the VBA implementation was constrained by sequential execution and tight Excel integration, making it unsuitable for large-scale parametric studies or modern computational workflows.

\subsection{Modernization of the LIQLEV Model}
To address the VBA implementation's limitations and meet the FY25 Drop Tower LLR Study requirements, LIQLEV was reimplemented in Python as two tightly integrated modules: a core transient solver (\texttt{liqlev\_simulation}) and a high-throughput 2D geometry-sweep driver (\texttt{liqlev\_sweep.py}). The modernization delivers high-accuracy thermodynamic property evaluation, support for arbitrary time-dependent vent profiles, robust numerical safeguards, and efficient parallel processing across thousands of configurations.

\subsubsection{Thermodynamic Integration via CoolProp}
The original VBA code relied on hardcoded property lookups and outdated curve fits (primarily for hydrogen) that limited flexibility across cryogens and pressure regimes. The Python version integrates the open-source CoolProp library~\cite{CoolProp} to provide consistent, high-accuracy real-fluid properties for nitrogen, hydrogen, oxygen, and methane across any pressure range relevant to the application.

Dedicated wrapper functions (\texttt{Cpsat}, \texttt{DensitySat}, \texttt{EnthalpySat}, \texttt{LHoV}, \texttt{Tsat}, \texttt{Psat}) evaluate saturation properties at every time step via direct \texttt{PropsSI} calls. The saturation-curve slope $\left(dP/dT\right)_{\rm sat}$, essential for depressurization and flashing calculations, is computed via a central finite-difference scheme with $\Delta P = 0.2\,$kPa to ensure numerical stability near the critical point. Hydrogen retains the original high-fidelity polynomials (valid for 69--345\,kPa, or 10--50\,psia) for direct AS-203 replication; all other fluids use full CoolProp evaluations. Internal unit conversions (kPa $\leftrightarrow$ psia, K $\leftrightarrow$ Rankine, kg/m$^3$ $\leftrightarrow$ lbm/ft$^3$) are handled transparently within the wrappers. This architecture eliminates hardcoded tables, supports any cryogen, and enables seamless extension to additional fluids.

\subsubsection{Complex Vent Profile Implementation}
The legacy model supported only constant or linearly ramped vent rates. The Python implementation accepts arbitrary time-dependent mass-flow-rate profiles as paired arrays \texttt{tvmdot} (time, s) and \texttt{xvmdot} (mass flow, lbm/s), with linear interpolation via the \texttt{sli} function at every solver step.

For direct validation against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ test~\cite{tani2021pressure}, a piecewise profile was implemented in \texttt{get\_jaxa\_config}. The profile reproduces the rapid 0--15\,s stepped blowdown (four discrete steps from 0 to $\sim$0.065\,kg/s) followed by long-term decay to $\sim$0.010\,kg/s at 3600\,s, closely matching Figures~6 and 12 of the reference. This addition substantially improves predicted pressure decay---including the characteristic initial pressure recovery from delayed bulk boiling---as well as superheat evolution, $V_{\rm BL}$, and LLR relative to the original fixed-rate assumption.

A new ``bulk\_fake'' $\epsilon$ mode was introduced to emulate distributed volumetric boiling in large tanks: setting $\epsilon \approx 75$--$100$ effectively scales the vapor-generating surface area by the same factor, reproducing the experimentally observed $\sim$174\,mm (6.85\,in) interface displacement within the first few seconds.

\subsubsection{Parallel Sweeps and JIT Acceleration}
Large-scale parametric analyses across tank geometry, fill level, vent rate, and gravity level are enabled by two complementary acceleration strategies. First, the \texttt{LIQLEV-Python-mp} package distributes the diameter--height grid across configurable worker processes via \texttt{concurrent.futures.ProcessPoolExecutor}, with the worker count taken from the SLURM environment (single-node, $\sim$2000 geometries per vent rate). Second, the transient solver is JIT-compiled with Numba and paired with a 400-point pre-tabulated CoolProp saturation table, eliminating per-step CoolProp calls. Together these deliver $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the original pure-Python loop on a representative 20\,s integration ($\sim$45\,s reduced to $\sim$1.1\,s per case), without measurable loss of accuracy: every physical signal agrees with the reference solver to better than 1 part in $10^6$.

For each diameter--height combination (50 diameters $\times$ 40 heights = 2000 geometries per vent rate), the full transient simulation runs with an early-termination check that halts execution the instant $h_2 \geq h_{\rm tank}$ (with 0.9999 tolerance for floating-point safety). LLR values are extracted at fixed snapshots ($t = 5\,$s, $t = 20\,$s or end-of-run, and global peak) via \texttt{np.interp}. Results are stored as 2D NumPy grids and exported to Excel workbooks (one per vent rate), with separate sheets for each snapshot, peak rise, and overfill flag. Contour plots are generated with \texttt{contourf}; hatched regions denote lid-impingement geometries, and a $50.8\,$mm (2.0\,in) target-rise contour with a baseline-geometry marker are overlaid for rapid design-space visualization.

Additional solver-robustness features include safeguards against zero driving force (\texttt{ak3} $\approx 0$), secant-method divide-by-zero protection, and extensive diagnostic outputs (superheat, vapor generation rate in kg/s, superficial vapor velocity, cumulative vapor mass, boundary-layer thickness, and instantaneous $\epsilon$). These collectively enable evaluation of thousands of configurations while preserving full traceability and numerical stability.

The resulting computational framework is a modern, extensible, and production-ready replacement for the legacy code, and it directly supports the experimental test-matrix optimization presented in Section~\ref{sec:exp_design_parameter_sweeps}.

The modernized framework retains the original one-dimensional piston-like displacement assumption: all generated vapor (boundary-layer plus flashing) is treated as uniformly distributed across the tank cross-section. The bulk\_fake mode is a practical engineering approximation for large-scale tanks in which distributed volumetric boiling dominates; future work will replace it with an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term while preserving the wall boundary-layer formulation.

\section{Model Verification and Validation}
\label{sec:vandv}

Model fidelity was established through two complementary tracks: a quantitative validation against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization experiment, and a code-to-code cross-validation against three-dimensional VOF CFD. Together they confirm that the modernized LIQLEV framework produces physically meaningful predictions in both the large-tank (bulk-flashing-dominated) and small-tank (boundary-layer-dominated) regimes.

\subsection{JAXA Large-Scale LH\texorpdfstring{$_2$}{2} Validation}
\label{sec:jaxa_validation}

Validation was performed against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ large-scale venting experiment reported by Tani et al.~\cite{tani2021pressure}. The dedicated module \texttt{tani\_validation.py} reproduces the experimental piecewise-cubic vent mass-flow profile (rapid stepped blowdown over $0$--$15$\,s followed by long-term decay), shown in Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_vent_profile}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/vent_rate.png}
\caption{Input vent-rate profile (kg/s) used for JAXA validation. The piecewise-cubic fit reproduces the rapid stepped blowdown (0--15\,s) and subsequent long-term decay observed experimentally.}
\label{fig:jaxa_vent_profile}
\end{figure}

When exercised with the standard height-dependent $\epsilon$ formulation ($\epsilon(t) = \pi D h(t)/[\pi D h(t) + \pi D^2/4]$), the model substantially under-predicts both the initial vapor generation rate and the measurable LLR. The discrepancy arises because, in the large-volume JAXA tank ($V_t = 30\,\text{m}^3$, $D = 2.3\,\text{m}$) at high fill fraction ($\approx 82\,\%$), rapid depressurization drives distributed bulk flashing throughout the superheated liquid column rather than confining vapor production to the thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted sidewall.

To reproduce the observed dynamics, a high effective $\epsilon$ was introduced via a ``bulk\_fake'' mode (constant $\epsilon$ in the range $75$--$100$). Physically, $\epsilon = N$ scales the effective vapor-generating surface area to $N$ times the actual wetted sidewall area, emulating the dominant bulk contribution.

The resulting pressure-decay predictions are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_pressure}. The bulk\_fake modes (especially $\epsilon = 75$ and $\epsilon = 100$) closely match the experimental data, capturing both the initial pressure recovery caused by delayed bulk boiling and the subsequent near-equilibrium depressurization. The height-dependent mode under-predicts the early vapor generation rate; its curve is largely obscured behind the $\epsilon = 1.0$ line and is therefore not visible on the plot.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/pressure_decay_full.png}
\caption{Simulated ullage pressure decay versus experimental data for the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ test. Bulk\_fake modes ($\epsilon = 50$, $75$, $100$) agree closely with the data; the height-dependent mode under-predicts early vapor generation (curve hidden behind the $\epsilon = 1.0$ line).}
\label{fig:jaxa_pressure}
\end{figure}

The LLR predictions (Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_level}) further confirm the necessity of the bulk\_fake adjustment. The height-dependent mode produces only a modest rise, whereas the bulk\_fake modes approach the experimentally observed displacement, detected when the TIE-04 temperature probe (located $6.422\,\text{m}$ from the tank bottom) became wetted. With an initial liquid height of $6.248\,\text{m}$, this corresponds to an interface displacement of $0.174\,\text{m}$ ($\approx 174\,\text{mm}$ or $6.85\,\text{in}$) within the first few seconds. Of the cases simulated, $\epsilon = 100$ provides the closest match---reaching $\sim 174\,\text{mm}$ at $t \approx 2.5\,\text{s}$ before plateauing around $\sim 185\,\text{mm}$. The $\epsilon = 75$ case plateaus near $\sim 152\,\text{mm}$, and $\epsilon = 50$ near $\sim 115\,\text{mm}$, both below the observed rise. The bulk-flashing-dominated regime in the JAXA tank is therefore best represented by an effective $\epsilon$ at the upper end of the explored range.

\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/liqlev_full.png}

These results confirm that migrating to CoolProp for high-accuracy real-fluid properties, combined with dynamic time-stepping and flexible $\epsilon$ modeling, eliminates the numerical artifacts of the legacy VBA implementation. The bulk\_fake mode (high constant $\epsilon$) provides a practical engineering approximation for large tanks in which bulk boiling dominates. Future development will add an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term to the original wall boundary-layer formulation, retaining the physically grounded foundation while introducing a first-principles bulk contribution.

\subsection{Computational Cross-Validation via Multiphase CFD}
\label{sec:cfd_crossval}

To test the one-dimensional interface-displacement assumption underlying LIQLEV, a hybrid analytical--CFD cross-validation was conducted in ANSYS Fluent using the VOF method to track the liquid--vapor interface under reduced gravity.

A one-way coupling strategy isolates hydrodynamic displacement effects while avoiding the modeling uncertainties of full heat-transfer and phase-change physics. The transient vapor mass generation rate $\dot{m}_{\rm vapor}(t)$ (boundary-layer plus interface contributions) computed by LIQLEV was exported and prescribed as a time-dependent mass-inlet boundary condition through a small orifice on the bottom wall of the cylindrical domain. The approach reproduces the net volumetric vapor addition without requiring an energy equation or detailed nucleation modeling in the CFD solver.

The representative simulation used a $91.4\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.610\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height cylindrical tank at 50\,\% liquid fill, subject to the gravity profile recorded during past GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility tests. High-resolution prism inflation layers along the tank walls resolve near-wall liquid climb; surface tension is captured via the continuum surface force (CSF) model. Turbulence is modeled with the Shear Stress Transport (SST) $k$-$\omega$ closure, with a fixed time step of $0.001\,\text{s}$ over a $5\,\text{s}$ total simulation.

The LIQLEV-predicted LLR for this configuration at the lowest vent rate ($1.36\,\text{g/s}$ or $0.003\,\text{lbm/s}$) is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_time_history}; the model predicts a smooth rise that reaches its maximum of approximately $81\,\text{mm}$ ($3.2\,\text{in}$) well within the 5-second low-gravity window.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]

The LIQLEV model predicts a maximum bulk LLR of $\sim 81\,\text{mm}$ ($3.2\,\text{in}$). The 3D CFD simulation shows the upper meniscus reaching $\approx 51\,\text{mm}$ ($2.0\,\text{in}$) at the cylinder axis---its lowest point, where an axial probe would touch the interface---and a radial-mean (volume-equivalent) rise of $\approx 59\,\text{mm}$. All three values are drawn as color-coded reference lines on the $t = 5\,\text{s}$ panel of Figure~\ref{fig:cfd_snapshots}. The LIQLEV prediction therefore sits $\sim 22\,\text{mm}$ above the volume-equivalent CFD measurement and $\sim 30\,\text{mm}$ above the centerline value.

The vapor-volume-fraction visualizations at $t = 0.01\,\text{s}$ and $t = 5.00\,\text{s}$ in Figure~\ref{fig:cfd_snapshots} reveal the source of the gap. At $0.01\,\text{s}$ the interface is still flat and vapor has just begun entering from the bottom orifice. By $5\,\text{s}$ a buoyant vapor plume has displaced liquid from the lower central column, and pronounced wall liquid climb has produced a curved upper meniscus---visible as the bulk vapor curling downward at the cylinder walls. LIQLEV's piston-like displacement assumption averages this three-dimensional structure into a single flat-interface height with vapor distributed uniformly across the cross-section; the CFD field captures both the local vapor injection at the orifice and the surface-tension-driven meniscus curvature, so any single point on the CFD interface lies below the LIQLEV-equivalent height.

LIQLEV therefore provides a conservatively high estimate of bulk-liquid displacement, appropriate for vent-line clearance sizing in pre-test design. In interpreting drop-tower probe data, the CFD centerline value bounds what an axial probe would measure, and the LIQLEV value bounds the worst-case displacement under the piston-like assumption.

\section{Experimental Design and Parameter Sweeps}
\label{sec:exp_design_parameter_sweeps}

The modernized LIQLEV framework is used to identify optimal test conditions across three NASA reduced-gravity facilities. The objective is to capture the peak liquid--vapor interface rise within each facility's available test duration while preventing lid impingement.

The candidate platforms were:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NASA GRC 2.2-Second Drop Tower:} $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g quality with a highly constrained $2.2\,\text{s}$ test window, requiring rapid interface dynamics to observe the initial transient.
    \item \textbf{NASA GRC 5-Second Zero-Gravity Facility:} $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g quality, with sufficient duration for boundary-layer growth and attainment of the peak LLR.
    \item \textbf{Parabolic Flight Campaigns:} $\sim 17\,\text{s}$ of reduced gravity ($\sim 10^{-2}$ to $10^{-3}\,$g with typical g-jitter), permitting longer-term thermodynamic stabilization and quasi-steady boiling.
\end{itemize}

Facility-specific gravity histories are incorporated through a custom \texttt{gravity\_function} callback. For the 5-second zero-gravity facility, accelerometer data from prior drop-tower tests (normalized time, axial acceleration) are converted to m/s$^2$ (ft/s$^2$ internally). A constant hold value of $\sim 1.4 \times 10^{-3}\,$g is appended past the last measured point so each simulation can be integrated beyond the nominal $5\,\text{s}$ window out to $20\,\text{s}$. This post-cutoff segment is a deliberate \textit{hypothetical} extension---the drop tower terminates microgravity at $5\,\text{s}$---used to verify that the predicted peak LLR within the real $5\,\text{s}$ window approaches its asymptotic value. The shaded region beyond $5\,\text{s}$ in all time-history figures marks this extrapolation.

The extension is essential for determining whether the peak LLR is attained \textit{within} the $5\,\text{s}$ microgravity window or only later. Comparing the time of peak rise against the rigid facility limit identifies tank geometries, fill fractions, and vent rates that capture the complete transient---from initial meniscus reorientation through sustained boundary-layer-driven rise---before the end of the test window without lid impingement.

\subsection{Governing Parameters}
The parameter sweep quantified the coupled effects of geometry, thermodynamics, venting, and gravity level on transient LLR. The following variables were varied across the full matrix:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tank Geometries and Aspect Ratios ($L/D$):} The primary grid consists of 50 diameter values ($61$--$305\,\text{mm}$, or $0.2$--$1.0\,\text{ft}$) and 40 height values ($91$--$762\,\text{mm}$, or $0.3$--$2.5\,\text{ft}$), yielding 2000 geometries per vent rate with aspect ratios $L/D \approx 2$--$12$. Targeted smaller-scale cases were also evaluated, including Skinner tanks of $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2$--$0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter at fixed $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2\,\text{ft}$) height, plus miniature cylindrical tanks matching Labus et al.\ ($60\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$ height).

    \item \textbf{Gravity Levels:} $10^{-3}\,$g, $10^{-4}\,$g, and $10^{-5}\,$g, bracketing the operational envelopes of all three facilities. Each level is implemented either as a constant or via piecewise \texttt{gravity\_function} callbacks.

    \item \textbf{Initial Fill Levels:} Liquid volume fractions from $20\,\%$ to $80\,\%$. Detailed matrices typically span $25$--$65\,\%$ for adequate optical visibility while maintaining margin against lid impingement.

    \item \textbf{Vent Flow Rates:} Constant rates scaled with tank size. For tanks of $61\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2\,\text{ft}$) diameter and larger (including the $0.61\,\text{m}$ Skinner series), four discrete rates between $1.36$ and $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$) were selected. These rates generate measurable LLR while keeping the interface below the tank lid at the chosen fill fractions. Proportionally smaller vent rates were applied to the Labus-scale ($60\,\text{mm}$ $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$) tanks to preserve comparable thermodynamic behavior across scales.
\end{enumerate}

Nitrogen serves as the baseline fluid for the primary geometric sweeps because it is the planned working fluid for the drop-tower experiments. This choice enables rapid computation, provides direct correlation with the planned hardware, and offers excellent optical transparency for high-speed imaging. Separate hydrogen runs were performed against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ data to confirm model fidelity for flight-relevant cryogens.

\subsection{Optimization Criteria}
The optimization process identifies tank geometries, initial fill fractions, and vent rates that maximize scientific return within each facility's rigid time constraint while preventing liquid impingement on the tank lid. Scientific return is defined as the ability to optically capture the complete liquid--vapor interface transient---from rapid meniscus reorientation through sustained boundary-layer-driven rise to eventual stabilization---under high-quality microgravity conditions.

The two primary optimization criteria are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The peak liquid--vapor interface height must be attained \textit{within} the available reduced-gravity duration and must produce an optically measurable rise of at least $50.8\,\text{mm}$ ($2\,\text{in}$). This minimum threshold ensures the thermodynamic signal exceeds residual g-jitter and imaging-system noise, enabling clean capture of the complete transient via high-speed imaging.
    \item The initial fill fraction must be constrained such that cumulative vapor generation (from both the free interface and wall thermal boundary layer) and the associated volumetric swelling keep the interface below the tank ceiling: $Z_{\rm int}(t) < Z_{\rm tank}$ for all $t \leq t_{\rm facility}$.
\end{itemize}

In microgravity the liquid--vapor interface rapidly transitions from a flat 1\,$g$ surface to a highly curved meniscus whose equilibrium shape is set by the contact angle and surface-energy minimization. Superimposed venting drives upward bulk motion through distributed bubble generation and displacement within the liquid column.

The Python implementation tracks the instantaneous interface location $Z_{\rm int}(t)$ (denoted internally as \texttt{h2} or \texttt{zht2}) via the discrete relation
\[
Z_{\rm int}(t) = Z_{\rm int}(t-\Delta t) + \frac{\Delta V_{\rm BL} + \Delta m_{\rm liq}/\rho_{\rm liq}}{A_c}\,\Delta t,
\]
where $\Delta V_{\rm BL}$ is the incremental vapor volume generated in the wall thermal boundary layer, $\Delta m_{\rm liq}$ is the mass of liquid flashed to vapor, $\rho_{\rm liq}$ is the saturated liquid density, and $A_c = \pi D^2/4$ is the tank cross-sectional area. This formulation embodies the one-dimensional piston-like displacement assumption central to the original LIQLEV boundary-layer model.

An early-termination flag halts each simulation the instant $Z_{\rm int} \ge Z_{\rm tank}$, records the overfill condition, and excludes that geometry from further analysis. LLR values are extracted at fixed snapshots ($t=5\,\text{s}$, $t=20\,\text{s}$ or end-of-run, and the global peak) via linear interpolation. These multi-time results, together with the overfill flag, are exported to structured \texttt{.xlsx} grids. This framework enables automated contour plotting with hatched regions denoting invalid (lid-impingement) configurations, supporting rapid down-selection of viable experimental test articles.

\subsection{Influence of Gravity Level on Liquid Level Rise}
Background acceleration exhibits a strong, non-linear inverse relationship with maximum LLR. At $10^{-3}\,$g, residual buoyancy promotes partial vapor bubble stratification and departure, shortening the residence time of vapor in the bulk liquid column. As gravity decreases to $10^{-5}\,$g (representative of the NASA GRC drop towers), bubble departure diameter grows and the relative rise velocity approaches zero. Vapor generated in the wall boundary layer and at the interface stays entrained, acting as distributed void volume that inflates the liquid column.

This mechanism is captured in the model through the boundary-layer vapor volume $V_{\rm BL}$ and the interface velocity:
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\left(\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt} + \frac{1}{\rho_l}\frac{dm_l}{dt}\right),
\]
where $A_c = \pi D^2/4$. The most aggressive LLR therefore occurs at the lowest gravity levels.

Variable-gravity parabolic profiles ($0.08\,$g and $0.01\,$g low-$g$ plateaus) were also evaluated and confirm that the full transient can be captured during the low-$g$ segment, with rises of $13$--$50\,\text{mm}$ ($0.5$--$2\,\text{in}$) or larger depending on vent rate and fill. The 2.2- and 5-second drop-tower environments share the same ultra-low gravity quality but lack the duration for the interface to reach its asymptotic maximum; higher vent rates are therefore required to generate a measurable rise within the rigid time window.

\subsection{Plateaued vs.\ Sustained-Growth Regimes}
\label{sec:plateaued_vs_sustained}

The transient time histories (Figures~\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories}--\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories_75}) reveal two qualitatively different regimes at fixed tank geometry, fluid, and gravity profile, separated only by vent rate. Low-vent-rate configurations approach a clear asymptote within $2$--$3\,\text{s}$; high-vent-rate configurations exhibit nearly monotonic interface rise that persists through the end of the $20\,\text{s}$ integration. The distinction is intrinsic to LIQLEV under sustained low-gravity venting rather than a numerical artifact, and it dictates which configurations are admissible as test articles.

The interface velocity in LIQLEV is the sum of two source terms:
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\left(\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt} + \frac{1}{\rho_l}\frac{dm_l}{dt}\right).
\]
Both $dV_{\rm BL}/dt$ (boundary-layer vapor generation) and $|dm_l/dt|/\rho_l$ (bulk flashing) scale with the instantaneous depressurization rate $|dP/dt|$ through the Clausius--Clapeyron relation. Vapor departure from the boundary layer is governed by the buoyancy term $a_1 \propto \sqrt{g(\rho_l - \rho_v)/\rho_l}$, which scales with the square root of the local gravity level: at $\sim 1.4 \times 10^{-3}\,$g (the held value past $5\,\text{s}$), $a_1$ is reduced by a factor of $\sim$27 relative to its $1\,$g value, sharply throttling buoyant bubble departure.

Two competing balances determine plateau formation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Plateaued regime} (low vent rate): $|dP/dt|$ is small enough that ullage repressurization from boundary-layer evaporation drives the system to quasi-steady state within the $5\,\text{s}$ window. Vapor generation balances venting, the bulk superheat decays, $V_{\rm BL}$ stabilizes, and $dh/dt \to 0$. For the recommended $91\,\text{mm}$ tank at $1.36$--$1.81\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.004\,\text{lbm/s}$), the value at $t = 5\,\text{s}$ is within $\sim$2\,\% of the asymptotic value (Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_time_history}).

    \item \textbf{Sustained-growth regime} (high vent rate): $|dP/dt|$ remains large because the tank pressure has not yet decayed to the target final value (e.g., for the $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$), 25\,\% fill case, $P = 45\,\text{kPa}$ at $t = 20\,\text{s}$, still above the $34\,\text{kPa}$ ($5\,\text{psia}$) target). The wall boundary layer continues producing vapor faster than buoyancy can remove it; the rising interface enlarges the wetted-wall area $\pi D h(t)$, providing positive feedback that further accelerates $dV_{\rm BL}/dt$. The interface velocity therefore does not relax to zero during the simulated window: for the same $2.72\,\text{g/s}$, 25\,\% fill case, $dh/dt$ \textit{increases} from $\sim 10^{-2}\,\text{ft/s}$ at $t = 10\,\text{s}$ to $\sim 8 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{ft/s}$ at $t = 20\,\text{s}$---an order-of-magnitude acceleration over the second half of the integration. These solutions remain bounded only by the vent-profile termination at $20\,\text{s}$ or by tank fill-up.
\end{itemize}

Physically, sustained-growth solutions correspond to drop-tower configurations in which the experiment captures only the early transient of an evolving boundary layer, not a true thermodynamic peak. They are excluded from the recommended test matrix (Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix}) because the $5\,\text{s}$ window cannot capture a representative maximum. This regime distinction also explains the marker convention in Figures~\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories}--\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories_75}: the value at $t = 5\,\text{s}$ (the experimentally measurable peak) is reported rather than the global maximum, because any peak occurring in the shaded extrapolated region beyond $5\,\text{s}$ is unreachable under the actual facility duty cycle.


\subsection{Tank Aspect Ratio Effects (5-Second Facility)}
A parametric sweep across more than 2000 cylindrical geometries (50 diameters from $60\,\text{mm}$ to $305\,\text{mm}$, or $0.197$--$1.0\,\text{ft}$, and 40 heights from $100\,\text{mm}$ to $762\,\text{mm}$, or $0.328$--$2.5\,\text{ft}$) was executed at $10^{-5}\,$g using the gravity profile extracted from prior NASA GRC drop-tower accelerometer data. Past the last measured point at $5\,\text{s}$, the acceleration is held constant for the $20\,\text{s}$ snapshot extrapolation. The height-dependent $\epsilon$ formulation is used throughout.

Candidate dimensions are down-selected against two practical hardware constraints---maximum diameter $\sim 305\,\text{mm}$ ($1\,\text{ft}$) and maximum height $\sim 0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2\,\text{ft}$), set by existing drop-capsule envelopes---while still encompassing the Labus-scale miniature ($60\,\text{mm}$ $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$).

The optimal performance region in the contour plots is the band between the red $50.8\,\text{mm}$ ($2\,\text{in}$) measurable-rise contour and the hatched overfill zones: tanks that produce an optically measurable rise (above the red contour) while remaining below the tank lid (outside the hatched regions) for practical fill fractions and vent rates. Smaller-diameter tanks deliver higher interface velocities because any incremental vapor volume $\Delta V_{\rm vapor}$ produces a larger displacement in the reduced cross-section:
\[
\Delta Z = \frac{\Delta V_{\rm vapor}}{A_c}.
\]

Four representative geometries within this optimal band were examined in detail:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $305\,\text{mm}$ ($1.0\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height, $L/D = 2$
    \item $229\,\text{mm}$ ($0.75\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D \approx 2.67$
    \item $152\,\text{mm}$ ($0.5\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D = 4$
    \item $91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D \approx 6.67$
\end{itemize}

Several trends emerge. The $305\,\text{mm}$-diameter tank is too wide: it provides excessive margin but produces insufficient rise within the 5-second window for most conditions. The smaller-diameter Skinner-style tanks ($61$--$91\,\text{mm}$) generate much stronger rise signals, though they often require greater height to prevent impingement at higher fills. Increasing the fill fraction expands the usable region, with the best overall balance near $50\,\%$ fill; at $75\,\%$ fill the hatched overfill regions grow substantially, reducing the viable configuration space.

The optimal test conditions therefore lie near $50\,\%$ fill at diameters between $\sim 91\,\text{mm}$ and $\sim 122\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3$--$0.4\,\text{ft}$). Diameters in the $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ range at $0.61\,\text{m}$ height perform particularly well across the selected vent rates, falling cleanly between the red measurable-rise contour and the hatched overfill regions.

\subsection{Facility-Specific Performance and Optimal Test Matrix}

\textbf{Parabolic Flight:}
The facility provides approximately $17\,\text{s}$ of reduced gravity, nominally $\sim 10^{-2}\,$g with typical g-jitter. Reference simulations were performed for the existing parabolic-flight test tank of~\cite{Opitz2021} (vacuum-jacketed 304L stainless-steel dewar, 100\,L LN$_2$ capacity, $620\,\text{kPa}$ ($90\,\text{psig}$) maximum pressure). Using conservative acceleration magnitudes ($\sqrt{g_x^2 + g_y^2 + g_z^2}$), LIQLEV predicts rises of $\gtrsim 76\,\text{mm}$ ($3\,\text{in}$) during the low-$g$ plateau. However, persistent lateral acceleration components ($10^{-2}$ to $5\times 10^{-3}\,$g) from the parabolic maneuver and attitude corrections excite unidirectional sloshing and asymmetric interfacial modes that contaminate the purely thermodynamic, boundary-layer-driven LLR signal of interest---consistent with prior parabolic-slosh observations in the literature. Parabolic flight was therefore not advanced as a test venue.


\textbf{GRC 2.2-Second Drop Tower:}
The facility operates over a gravity range of $\sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}\,$g. Parametric investigations across tank diameters, aspect ratios, initial fill fractions, and vent rates show that the $2.2\,\text{s}$ test window is too short for the interface to reach its maximum thermodynamic rise. Even with high initial fill fractions ($\sim 75$--$80\,\%$) and aggressive vent rates ($0.113$--$0.340\,\text{kg/s}$, or $0.25$--$0.75\,\text{lbm/s}$) intended to drive rapid interface motion within the first $1.5$--$2\,\text{s}$, only the early transient of boundary-layer vapor generation and liquid displacement is captured; sustained boiling does not develop the peak rise within the available time.

The GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, by contrast, offers both a longer test duration and a lower residual gravity level ($\sim 10^{-5}\,$g), enabling a substantially higher measurable peak rise once the configuration is optimized. Achieving a comparable peak in the 2.2-second facility would require even narrower tanks and higher vent rates than are viable in the 5-second facility---geometries that no longer fit within the existing drop-capsule envelope.

\textbf{GRC 5-Second Zero-Gravity Facility:}
The facility operates at $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g. Parametric sweeps spanning thousands of geometries (diameters $61$--$305\,\text{mm}$, heights $91$--$762\,\text{mm}$), initial fill fractions, vent rates, and $\epsilon$ schedules identify this platform as the optimal test venue. It provides the right balance of high-quality microgravity and sufficient test duration for the wall boundary-layer vapor generation and resulting liquid displacement to develop fully, allowing the interface to attain its maximum thermodynamic rise---unlike the time-constrained 2.2-second facility. The low residual gravity further enhances measurable displacement by suppressing buoyant bubble departure and extending vapor residence time, producing substantially larger rises than the higher-$g$ environment typical of parabolic flight.

Recommended configurations use a small-diameter cylindrical tank ($91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height, $L/D \approx 6.67$) with initial fill fractions of $50$--$75\,\%$ and constant vent rates in the $1.36$--$2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$) range. Under these conditions, LIQLEV predicts peak rises between $\sim 80$ and $\sim 155\,\text{mm}$ ($\sim 3.1$--$6.1\,\text{in}$), attained between $\sim 3$ and $5\,\text{s}$. The lower-vent / $50\,\%$ fill cases plateau cleanly within the $5\,\text{s}$ window; the highest-vent / $75\,\%$ fill cases reach the tank lid before the predicted peak and serve to bound the operating envelope.

The proposed experimental test matrix is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix}. Twelve conditions span two fill levels ($50\,\%$ and $75\,\%$) and three vent-rate categories (Low / Med / High) at both $1\,$g (ground baselines) and $10^{-5}\,$g (drop-tower microgravity), all with liquid nitrogen (LN$_2$) and gaseous nitrogen (GN$_2$). Each test is performed twice for repeatability assessment, yielding approximately 15 drops total once shake-down repeats are excluded.

Vent rates are specified as qualitative \emph{Low / Med / High} categories rather than as the LIQLEV-predicted numerical values ($1.36$, $2.04$, and $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ as candidate centerlines for the three bins). Whether the test apparatus can deliver these specific flow rates---and hold them stable across the $5\,\text{s}$ drop window---is itself a hardware-validation question that the $1\,$g shake-down tests (Tests~1--6) must answer before the microgravity drops (Tests~7--12). Tests~1--6 therefore serve a dual purpose: they calibrate the Low / Med / High categories to numerical mass-flow rates for the actual hardware, and they provide $1\,$g reference data for direct comparison against the matched-condition microgravity drops. The final mapping of Low / Med / High to specific mass-flow rates will be reported alongside the experimental data.

\subsection{Effect of Epsilon (\texorpdfstring{$\epsilon$}{epsilon}) and Model Sensitivity}
The boil-off partitioning parameter $\epsilon$ is the instantaneous fraction of total vapor mass generated in the wall thermal boundary layer relative to that generated at the liquid--vapor free surface. In the modernized LIQLEV, $\epsilon$ is evaluated at every solver time step. In the default height-dependent mode (\texttt{neps} = 0), it follows directly from the geometric wetted-area ratio per the Moran formulation:

\[
\epsilon(t) = \frac{\pi D \, h(t)}{\pi D \, h(t) + \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4}},
\]

where $D$ is the tank diameter and $h(t)$ is the instantaneous liquid height. This expression assumes vapor production scales proportionally with the respective heat-transfer areas (sidewall wetted surface versus free interface).

A sensitivity study confirmed the dominant influence of $\epsilon$ at low gravity. Comparing the time-dependent $\epsilon(t)$ schedule derived from the evolving wetted-area ratio against constant-$\epsilon$ cases ($\epsilon = 0.4$, $0.625$, and $0.8$) at identical geometry, fill, and vent rate, peak LLR differs by up to $30$--$40\,\%$. The effect is most pronounced at $10^{-5}\,$g, where reduced buoyancy keeps wall-generated vapor bubbles entrained longer, increasing the volumetric displacement of the liquid column. These differences, together with the temporal evolution of $\epsilon(t)$ itself, are illustrated in the epsilon-sensitivity time-history and contour plots compiled for each reduced-gravity facility.

\subsection{Effect of Tank Size on Meniscus Reorientation Time}

Additional 3D VOF CFD simulations were performed using the same modeling framework (CSF model, near-wall prism layers, SST $k$-$\omega$ turbulence closure) to assess the effect of tank scale on liquid--vapor interface reorientation. Two geometries of direct relevance to the experimental matrix were examined: the Skinner-scale tank ($61\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height) and the Labus-scale tank ($60\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$ height). Each geometry was simulated at three gravity levels ($10^{-3}\,$g, $10^{-4}\,$g, $10^{-5}\,$g) to quantify the time required for the interface to reorient from a flat 1\,$g$ surface to its equilibrium curved meniscus.

The results (Figures~\ref{fig:cfd_meniscus_0p2ft} and~\ref{fig:cfd_meniscus_6cm}) reveal two trends. First, reorientation time increases markedly with decreasing gravity. Second, the smaller Labus-scale tank reaches a stable meniscus significantly faster than the $61\,\text{mm}$ tank at any given gravity, consistent with the reduced Bond number (the ratio of gravitational to surface-tension forces) in smaller geometries and lower gravity, where surface tension dominates more rapidly.

The CFD-derived meniscus formation times provide quantitative insight into pre-venting interface dynamics. For the recommended $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ height tanks, the simulations indicate that complete reorientation may require more time than is available before venting begins in the GRC 5-second facility. Whether full pre-venting meniscus stabilization is required to meet the scientific objectives remains an open question, to be resolved by the forthcoming physical experiments. The results nonetheless confirm that the selected tank scale remains well suited to capture the targeted boundary-layer-driven LLR once venting commences.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\paragraph{Findings.} The legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model has been reimplemented as a parallelized, CoolProp-integrated Python framework with a Numba JIT-compiled solver that delivers $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the original pure-Python loop while reproducing the reference solution to better than 1 part in $10^6$. The modernized model has been validated against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization experiment: a constant $\epsilon$ in the range $75$--$100$ (\texttt{bulk\_fake} mode) reproduces the experimentally observed $174\,\text{mm}$ ($6.85\,\text{in}$) interface rise within the first few seconds, with $\epsilon \approx 100$ providing the closest match. Three-dimensional VOF CFD cross-validation confirms that LIQLEV provides a conservatively high estimate of bulk-liquid displacement, exceeding the volume-equivalent flat-interface CFD rise by $\sim 22\,\text{mm}$ at the $91\,\text{mm} \times 0.61\,\text{m}$ scale. Parametric sweeps across more than 2000 cylindrical geometries identify the GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility as the optimal test venue, with a recommended test article of $91\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ height ($L/D \approx 6.67$), nitrogen working fluid, and a 12-condition test matrix spanning $50\,\%$ and $75\,\%$ initial fill at three vent-rate categories (Low / Med / High, calibrated to LIQLEV-predicted target rates of $1.36$--$2.72\,\text{g/s}$ during the $1\,$g shake-down phase). Under these conditions the predicted peak rise spans $\sim 80$--$155\,\text{mm}$ ($\sim 3.1$--$6.1\,\text{in}$), attained between $\sim 3$ and $5\,\text{s}$, comfortably within the facility's microgravity window for the lower-vent / $50\,\%$ fill cases.

\paragraph{Forward Plan.} The FY26+ drop-tower campaign will execute the matrix in Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix} using high-speed imaging, sidewall temperature probes, and calibrated ullage pressure transducers. The resulting data will close the validation loop on the modernized model, motivate development of an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term to retire the \texttt{bulk\_fake} engineering approximation, and establish the first sub-meter-scale, flight-relevant transient LLR data set for CFM correlation development. The 2.2-second drop tower was not pursued because the $2.2\,\text{s}$ test window is too short for the boundary-layer-driven interface rise to develop and reach a measurable peak under any geometry compatible with the existing drop-capsule envelope; parabolic flight was likewise set aside because lateral g-jitter contaminates the boundary-layer-driven signal.

\paragraph{Programmatic Impact.} The validated modeling framework and pre-test design directly support vent-system sizing for HLS-class vehicles, in-space cryogenic depots, and Mars transfer stages---all critically dependent on reliable microgravity vent operations to meet propellant residual and $\Delta v$ margin requirements. By replacing single-data-point reliance on the 1966 AS-203 flight with a sub-meter-scale ground experiment anchored by a verified, fast, and extensible analytical tool, the proposed campaign reduces a long-standing technical risk on the path from current CFM practice to long-duration human spaceflight. The Python implementation, including \texttt{LIQLEV-Python-mp} and the JIT-accelerated solver, is available from the authors upon request.
