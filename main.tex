\begin{abstract}
The management of cryogenic propellants under microgravity conditions present significant challenges, particularly the safe venting of vapor without entraining liquid. During pressure reduction, bulk boiling and wall boundary-layer phenomena can cause substantial rise of the liquid-vapor interface, potentially leading to liquid entrainment, propellant loss, and damage to the vent system.

This study modernizes the legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model, originally developed following the Saturn AS-203 flight experiment. The original Fortran and VBA implementation has been completely rewritten as a robust, multiprocessing Python framework integrated with CoolProp to enable high-accuracy real-fluid thermodynamic properties for multiple cryogens.

Extensive parametric sweeps were conducted across initial fill levels, vent flow rates, tank aspect ratios, and microgravity regimes from $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}\,$g to evaluate three candidate test platforms: the NASA GRC 2.2-second drop tower, the NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, and parabolic flight campaigns. Results show that the NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility offers the best balance of high-quality microgravity and sufficient test duration to fully capture the maximum measurable LLR. Optimal initial fill fractions were also identified to prevent lid impingement.

The resulting computational framework and recommended test matrix provide a critical baseline for future physical experiments. These efforts will yield high-quality data on liquid-vapor interface dynamics and support the development of improved correlations for cryogenic fluid management systems.
\end{abstract}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Cryogenic Fluid Management, Microgravity, Zero-Gravity Venting, Liquid Level Rise, Computational Fluid Dynamics, LIQLEV, Drop Tower Testing.

\section{Introduction}
As space exploration architectures increasingly depend on cryogenic propellants such as liquid hydrogen (LH$_2$), liquid oxygen (LO$_x$), and liquid methane (LCH$_4$), robust Cryogenic Fluid Management (CFM) technologies have become essential. In microgravity, buoyancy forces are greatly diminished, allowing surface tension to dominate fluid behavior and causing the liquid-vapor interface to become highly distorted.

When a cryogenic tank is vented to relieve internal pressure caused by environmental heat leaks, the rapid pressure reduction initiates thermodynamic flashing and bulk boiling. Vapor generated within the superheated liquid bulk and along the wetted tank walls forms bubbles that displace the surrounding liquid, causing significant rise of the liquid-vapor interface. If this two-phase mixture reaches the vent port, liquid entrainment can occur, resulting in substantial propellant loss and potential damage to the venting infrastructure.

Understanding and accurately predicting this transient liquid level rise (LLR) is critical for the design of safe and efficient vent systems and operational venting procedures. Early experimental investigations by Labus et al. \cite{Labus1972, Labus1974} conducted in the NASA Lewis Research Center 5-second zero-gravity facility provided foundational insights into the venting of refrigerants under reduced-gravity conditions. A major milestone was the Saturn S-IVB AS-203 flight experiment \cite{AS203_1970}, which provided the most comprehensive in-orbit data on liquid hydrogen behavior during venting and directly led to the development of the LIQLEV analytical boundary-layer model.

This paper presents the modernization of the LIQLEV model and its application to support the FY25 Drop Tower LLR Study. The primary objectives are to translate the legacy code into Python, expand its thermodynamic modeling capabilities, and perform comprehensive parametric sweeps of tank size, vent rate, fill level, and gravity level. These analyses identify optimal experimental conditions that enable a measurable maximum liquid-vapor interface height to be captured within the limited test durations of available reduced-gravity facilities: the NASA Glenn Research Center (GRC) 2.2-second drop tower, the GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, and parabolic flight campaigns.

\subsection{Need and Significance}
\label{sec:need_significance}

Long-duration human and robotic missions to the Moon and Mars depend on in-space cryogenic propellants for descent stages, ascent stages, transfer vehicles, and propellant depots. NASA's Moon-to-Mars architecture, the Human Landing System (HLS), and proposed nuclear thermal/electric propulsion stages all require storing and managing liquid hydrogen, oxygen, and methane for weeks to months in microgravity~\cite{Meyer2019, Hartwig2015}. Reliable vent operations that relieve tank ullage pressure without expelling liquid are an enabling capability for each of these missions.

When a cryogenic tank is vented to relieve pressurization caused by parasitic heat leak, the rapid pressure reduction induces bulk flashing and wall-driven boundary-layer boiling. The resulting two-phase swelling can drive the liquid--vapor interface into the vent line, producing liquid carryover, propellant residuals, and---in the worst case---damage to the vent system itself~\cite{Hastings2003, Hartwig2015}. Mission-level analyses of in-space depot architectures place propellant residuals and boil-off losses in the range of several percent per month under poorly characterized vent conditions~\cite{Plachta2014, Meyer2019}, directly degrading payload capacity and mission $\Delta v$ margin.

Despite the maturity of the underlying physics, predictive correlations for transient liquid level rise (LLR) under microgravity venting remain anchored to a single in-orbit data set: the Saturn S-IVB AS-203 LH$_2$ experiment of 1966~\cite{AS203_1970}. Subsequent ground campaigns---including the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization test~\cite{tani2021pressure} and ISS-based Zero Boil-Off Tank (ZBOT) experiments~\cite{Kassemi2018}---have advanced the understanding of self-pressurization and active pressure control, yet none directly resolve the boundary-layer-driven LLR transient at flight-relevant gravity levels in tanks of sub-meter scale. The gap is acute for HLS-class vehicles, where small ullage margins and aggressive vent duty cycles place LLR squarely on the critical path for vent-system sizing~\cite{NASA80NSSC24K1219}.

This paper addresses the gap in three steps. First, the legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model is reimplemented as a parallelized, CoolProp-integrated Python framework with a Numba just-in-time (JIT) compiled solver that delivers $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the prior pure-Python implementation, enabling thousand-case parametric sweeps in minutes rather than hours. Second, the modernized model is verified against the JAXA large-scale LH$_2$ data and cross-validated with three-dimensional volume-of-fluid (VOF) CFD. Third, the verified framework is used to down-select a recommended experimental test matrix for the NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility, providing pre-test predictions and instrumentation requirements for a planned drop-tower campaign that will yield the first sub-meter-scale, flight-relevant LLR data set for cryogenic fluid management correlation development.

\section{Background and Literature Review}

\subsection{Early Experimental Investigations}
Foundational experimental studies on zero-gravity venting were conducted by Labus et al.~\cite{Labus1972, Labus1974} in the NASA Lewis Research Center 5-second zero-gravity facility. These tests examined the venting of cylindrical acrylic containers partially filled with initially saturated Refrigerants 11 (R11), octofluorocyclobutane (RC318), and n-butane (R600). The test liquids exhibited near-zero contact angles on the acrylic container walls, resulting in hemispherical liquid-vapor interfaces under zero-gravity conditions.

Representative sequences from these experiments illustrate the strong dependence of boiling behavior on vent rate. At a relatively high vent rate of approximately 1.0 ullage volume per second (using Refrigerant C318), significant bulk boiling was observed shortly after venting began. Vapor generation increased rapidly near the liquid-vapor interface, causing the interface to rise noticeably toward the vent and producing visible vapor bubbles within the bulk liquid (Figure~\ref{fig:labus_1p0}).

In contrast, at a lower vent rate of 0.5 ullage volume per second (again using Refrigerant C318), no bulk boiling occurred. Vaporization was confined almost entirely to the liquid-vapor free surface, with the interface remaining relatively stable and no significant bubble formation in the bulk liquid (Figure~\ref{fig:labus_0p5}).

These observations demonstrated that both interfacial evaporation at the liquid-vapor free surface and bulk boiling within the liquid can occur during venting, with the transition between the two regimes governed primarily by the vent rate. Interfacial mass transfer was shown to play a significant role in determining the ullage pressure response, particularly at low vent rates. Analytical comparisons using a conduction-based model assuming a flat interface further highlighted the importance of accounting for mass transfer effects to accurately predict pressure decay.

\subsection{The Saturn S-IVB AS-203 Flight Experiment}
The most comprehensive in-orbit data on cryogenic liquid behavior during venting were obtained during the Saturn S-IVB AS-203 mission launched on 5 July 1966 \cite{AS203_1970}. The experiment featured three distinct rapid depressurization sequences of the liquid hydrogen tank, executed with both the Continuous Vent System (CVS) and the Non-Propulsive Vent (NPV) system. These controlled pressure reductions of several psi occurred while the vehicle remained in a low-gravity coast environment ($\sim10^{-4}$ to $10^{-5}$\,g).

The S-IVB LH$_2$ tank was instrumented with an extensive array of temperature diodes positioned at multiple axial stations along with dedicated liquid-vapor point sensors. Of particular importance was liquid-vapor sensor N052 (also denoted NO52), which was located at the same axial station as temperature diode C0347, positioned slightly above the nominal liquid-vapor interface (approximately 43\,\% fill level at station 438). Sensor N052 was a variable-resistance type device similar to those used on the Centaur vehicle. It proved to be the only liquid-vapor sensor that operated reliably; its wetting and drying indications agreed closely with the temperature response of the co-located diode C0347. The remaining liquid-vapor sensors exhibited anomalous behavior, apparently trapping liquid once wetted and thereby producing erroneous persistent wet indications.

During the second and third depressurization events, sensor N052—initially residing in the ullage—registered a transition to liquid contact, providing direct evidence of substantial upward displacement of the liquid-vapor interface. Temperature diode readings near the initial interface revealed regions of superheated liquid followed by rapid cooling associated with boiling onset. Quantitative analysis of the flight data demonstrated that the observed liquid level rise substantially exceeded that attributable to simple flashing or volumetric expansion alone, scaling strongly with both the magnitude and rate of pressure reduction (Figure~\ref{fig:as203_llr_vs_dp}).

These unique low-gravity venting observations established that vapor generation within the thermal boundary layer along the wetted tank sidewall, together with bulk flashing, produced significant bulk liquid displacement. The AS-203 flight data therefore provided the definitive validation benchmark and served as the primary motivation for the development of the original LIQLEV analytical boundary-layer model, which explicitly accounts for wall-driven boil-off and its contribution to interface rise.

\subsection{Development of the Original LIQLEV Model}
Following the AS-203 flight, the LIQLEV (Liquid Level) boundary-layer model was developed in Fortran to predict the contribution of wall boundary-layer boil-off to bulk liquid displacement. The model, fully documented in Appendix B of NASA CR-109847, focused on vapor generation within a thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted tank sidewall and its effect on the upward motion of the liquid-vapor interface.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/saturn_boundary_layer_model.png}
\caption{Bubble boundary-layer model developed for the AS-203 liquid hydrogen tank \cite{AS203_1970}. The model treats vapor generation within a thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted sidewall, with bubbles contributing to bulk liquid displacement.}
\label{fig:liqlev_boundary_layer}
\end{figure}

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_boundary_layer}, the model treats the sidewall as a surface on which a growing vapor film forms; bubbles nucleate, grow, and detach within this film, displacing liquid and raising the interface. The governing physics are expressed through unsteady mass and energy balances on differential elements of the boundary layer (Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_mass_balance}), yielding the instantaneous vapor volume $V_{\rm BL}(t)$ and the resulting interface velocity
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt},
\]
where $A_c$ is the tank cross-sectional area. Parametric studies examined the sensitivity of liquid level rise to vent rate, tank diameter, gravity level, and bubble spacing.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/saturn_mass_balance.png}
\caption{Mass balance for a differential element of the unsteady thermal boundary layer in the original LIQLEV model \cite{AS203_1970}}
\label{fig:liqlev_mass_balance}
\end{figure}

In recent years, Moran \cite{MoranVBA} adapted the original Fortran code into an Excel Visual Basic for Applications (VBA) environment to support the Human Landing System (HLS) program. This adaptation required meticulous line-by-line debugging of the legacy source code from the poorly scanned NASA CR-109847 reports. Moran’s implementation successfully replicated the AS-203 flight conditions, replaced outdated hydrogen property curve fits with initial calls to the CoolProp library, and introduced a dynamic ratio $\epsilon$ representing the proportion of boil-off generated in the wall boundary layer relative to the free surface. This ratio was made dependent on the wetted wall area and interface area.

Although functional, the VBA version was limited by its sequential execution and tight integration within Excel, making it unsuitable for large-scale parametric studies and modern computational workflows.

\subsection{Modernization of the LIQLEV Model}
To address the limitations of the VBA implementation and fulfill the requirements of the FY25 Drop Tower LLR Study, the LIQLEV model was completely reimplemented in Python as two tightly integrated modules: a core transient solver (\texttt{liqlev\_simulation}) and a high-throughput 2D geometry sweep driver (\texttt{liqlev\_sweep.py}). This modernization provides several critical advancements, including high-accuracy thermodynamic property evaluation, support for arbitrary time-dependent vent profiles, robust numerical safeguards, and efficient parallel processing for thousands of configurations.

\subsubsection{Thermodynamic Integration via CoolProp}
The original VBA code relied on localized, hardcoded property lookups and outdated curve fits (primarily for hydrogen), which severely limited flexibility for other cryogens and broad pressure regimes. The Python version integrates the open-source \texttt{CoolProp} library \cite{CoolProp} to provide consistent, high-accuracy real-fluid properties for Nitrogen, Hydrogen, Oxygen, and Methane across any pressure range.

Dedicated wrapper functions (\texttt{Cpsat}, \texttt{DensitySat}, \texttt{EnthalpySat}, \texttt{LHoV}, \texttt{Tsat}, \texttt{Psat}) evaluate saturation properties at every time step using direct \texttt{PropsSI} calls. The critical saturation-curve slope $\left(dP/dT\right)_{\rm sat}$, essential for depressurization and flashing calculations, is computed dynamically via a central finite-difference scheme with $\Delta P = 0.2$\,kPa to ensure numerical stability near the critical point. Hydrogen retains its original high-fidelity polynomials (valid for 69--345\,kPa or 10--50\,psia) for exact replication of AS-203 conditions, while all other fluids use full CoolProp evaluations. Internal unit conversions (kPa $\leftrightarrow$ psia, K $\leftrightarrow$ Rankine, kg/m$^3$ $\leftrightarrow$ lbm/ft$^3$) are handled transparently within the wrappers. This architecture eliminates hardcoded tables, supports any cryogen, and enables seamless extension to future fluids.

\subsubsection{Complex Vent Profile Implementation}
The legacy model supported only constant or linearly ramped vent rates. The Python implementation was upgraded to accept arbitrary time-dependent vent mass-flow-rate profiles supplied as paired arrays \texttt{tvmdot} (time points in s) and \texttt{xvmdot} (mass flow in lbm/s). Linear interpolation via the \texttt{sli} function is performed at every solver step.

For direct validation against the JAXA $30\,m^3$ LH$_2$ large-scale test \cite{tani2021pressure}, a high-fidelity piecewise profile was implemented in \texttt{get\_jaxa\_config}. The profile reproduces the rapid 0--15\,s stepped blowdown (four discrete steps from 0 to $\sim$0.065\,kg/s) followed by long-term decay to $\sim$0.010\,kg/s at 3600\,s, closely matching the experimental data in Figures 6 and 12 of the reference. This modification significantly improves the fidelity of predicted pressure decay (including the characteristic initial pressure recovery due to delayed bulk boiling), superheat evolution, boundary-layer vapor volume, and liquid level rise compared with the original fixed-rate assumption.

A new ``bulk\_fake'' epsilon mode was introduced to emulate distributed volumetric boiling in large tanks: setting $\epsilon \approx 75$--$100$ effectively scales the vapor-generating surface area by the same factor, reproducing the experimentally observed $\sim$174\,mm (6.85\,in) interface displacement within the first few seconds.

\subsubsection{Parallel Sweeps and JIT Acceleration}
Large-scale parametric analyses varying tank geometry, fill level, vent rate, and gravity level were enabled by two complementary acceleration strategies. First, the \texttt{LIQLEV-Python-mp} package distributes the two-dimensional diameter--height grid across configurable Python worker processes via \texttt{concurrent.futures.ProcessPoolExecutor}, with the worker count taken from the SLURM environment (single-node, $\sim$2000 geometries per vent rate). Second, the transient solver itself was JIT-compiled with Numba and paired with a 400-point pre-tabulated CoolProp saturation property table, eliminating per-step CoolProp calls. The combined effect is a $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the original pure-Python loop on a representative 20\,s integration ($\sim$45\,s reduced to $\sim$1.1\,s per case), without measurable loss of accuracy: every physical signal agrees with the reference solver to better than 1 part in $10^6$.

For each diameter--height combination (50 diameters $\times$ 40 heights = 2000 geometries per vent rate), the full transient simulation is performed with an early-termination check that halts execution the instant $h_2 \ge h_{\rm tank}$ (with 0.9999 tolerance for floating-point safety). Liquid level rise values are extracted at fixed snapshots ($t = 5$\,s, $t = 20$\,s or end-of-run, and global peak) via \texttt{np.interp}. Results are stored as structured 2D NumPy grids and exported to Excel workbooks (one file per vent rate) with separate sheets for each snapshot, maximum rise, and overfill flag. Contour plots are automatically generated using \texttt{contourf} with hatched regions (\texttt{hatches=['XX']}) denoting lid-impingement geometries. A 50.8\,mm (2.0\,in) target-rise contour and baseline geometry marker are overlaid for rapid design-space visualization.

Additional solver robustness features include safeguards against zero driving force (\texttt{ak3} $\approx 0$), secant-method divide-by-zero protection, and extensive diagnostic outputs (superheat, vapor generation rate in kg/s, superficial vapor velocity, cumulative vapor mass, boundary-layer thickness, and instantaneous $\epsilon$). These enhancements collectively enable efficient evaluation of thousands of configurations while preserving full traceability and numerical stability.

The resulting computational framework provides a modern, extensible, and production-ready replacement for the legacy code, directly supporting the experimental test matrix optimization described in Section~\ref{sec:exp_design_parameter_sweeps}.

The modernized LIQLEV framework retains the original one-dimensional piston-like displacement assumption, whereby all vapor generated (boundary-layer plus flashing) is treated as uniformly distributed across the tank cross-section. The bulk\_fake mode provides a practical engineering approximation for large-scale tanks in which distributed volumetric boiling dominates; future work will replace this with an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term while preserving the wall boundary-layer formulation.

\section{Model Verification and Validation}
\label{sec:vandv}

Model fidelity was established through two complementary tracks: a quantitative validation against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization experiment, and a code-to-code cross-validation against three-dimensional volume-of-fluid (VOF) CFD. Together these establish that the modernized LIQLEV framework produces physically meaningful predictions across both the large-tank (bulk-flashing-dominated) and small-tank (boundary-layer-dominated) regimes.

\subsection{JAXA Large-Scale LH\texorpdfstring{$_2$}{2} Validation}
\label{sec:jaxa_validation}

Validation was performed against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ large-scale venting experiment reported by Tani et al.~\cite{tani2021pressure}. The dedicated module \texttt{tani\_validation.py} reproduces the experimental piecewise-cubic vent mass-flow profile (rapid stepped blowdown over $0$--$15$\,s followed by long-term decay), shown in Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_vent_profile}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/vent_rate.png}
\caption{Input vent-rate profile (kg/s) used for JAXA validation. The piecewise-cubic fit reproduces the rapid stepped blowdown (0--15\,s) and subsequent long-term decay observed experimentally.}
\label{fig:jaxa_vent_profile}
\end{figure}

When exercised with the standard height-dependent $\epsilon$ formulation ($\epsilon(t) = \pi D h(t)/[\pi D h(t) + \pi D^2/4]$), the model substantially under-predicts both the initial vapor generation rate and the measurable LLR. This discrepancy arises because, in the large-volume JAXA tank ($V_t = 30\,\text{m}^3$, $D = 2.3\,\text{m}$) at high fill fraction ($\approx 82\,\%$), the dominant vapor production mechanism during rapid depressurization is distributed bulk flashing throughout the entire superheated liquid column, rather than being confined to the thin thermal boundary layer along the wetted sidewall.

To reproduce the experimentally observed dynamics, a high effective $\epsilon$ was introduced via a ``bulk\_fake'' mode (constant $\epsilon$ in the range $75$--$100$). Physically, $\epsilon = N$ instructs the model to treat the effective vapor-generating surface area as $N$ times the actual wetted sidewall area---equivalent to scaling the liquid volume that participates in boiling by the same factor to emulate the dominant bulk contribution.

The resulting pressure-decay predictions are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_pressure}. The bulk\_fake modes (especially $\epsilon = 75$ and $\epsilon = 100$) closely match the experimental data, capturing both the initial pressure recovery caused by delayed bulk boiling and the subsequent near-equilibrium depressurization. The height-dependent mode under-predicts the early vapor generation rate; its curve is largely obscured behind the $\epsilon = 1.0$ line and is therefore not visible on the plot.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/pressure_decay_full.png}
\caption{Simulated ullage pressure decay versus experimental data for the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ test. Bulk\_fake modes ($\epsilon = 50$, $75$, $100$) agree closely with the data; the height-dependent mode under-predicts early vapor generation (curve hidden behind the $\epsilon = 1.0$ line).}
\label{fig:jaxa_pressure}
\end{figure}

The LLR predictions (Figure~\ref{fig:jaxa_level}) further confirm the necessity of the bulk\_fake adjustment. The height-dependent mode produces only a modest rise, whereas the bulk\_fake modes approach the experimentally observed displacement, detected when the TIE-04 temperature probe (located $6.422\,\text{m}$ from the tank bottom) became wetted. With an initial liquid height of $6.248\,\text{m}$, this corresponds to an interface displacement of $0.174\,\text{m}$ ($\approx 174\,\text{mm}$ or $6.85\,\text{in}$) within the first few seconds. Of the cases simulated, $\epsilon = 100$ provides the closest match---reaching $\sim 174\,\text{mm}$ at $t \approx 2.5\,\text{s}$ before plateauing around $185\,\text{mm}$. The $\epsilon = 75$ case plateaus near $150\,\text{mm}$, and $\epsilon = 50$ near $110\,\text{mm}$, both below the observed rise. The bulk-flashing-dominated regime in the JAXA tank is therefore best represented by an effective $\epsilon$ at the upper end of the explored range.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/tani_validation/liqlev_full.png}
\caption{Liquid level rise versus time for different $\epsilon$ modes in the JAXA validation case. Only the bulk\_fake modes reproduce the experimentally observed $\approx 174\,\text{mm}$ ($6.85\,\text{in}$) rise.}

These results confirm that the migration to \texttt{CoolProp} for high-accuracy real-fluid properties, combined with dynamic time-stepping and flexible $\epsilon$ modeling, eliminated the numerical artifacts of the legacy VBA implementation. The bulk\_fake mode (high constant $\epsilon$) provides a practical engineering approximation for large tanks in which bulk boiling dominates. Future development will incorporate an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term in addition to the original wall boundary-layer formulation, retaining the physically grounded boundary-layer foundation while supplying a first-principles bulk contribution.

\subsection{Computational Cross-Validation via Multiphase CFD}
\label{sec:cfd_crossval}

To assess the validity of the one-dimensional interface displacement assumptions underlying LIQLEV, a hybrid analytical--CFD cross-validation was conducted using three-dimensional multiphase CFD in ANSYS Fluent. The Volume-of-Fluid (VOF) method was employed to track the liquid--vapor interface evolution under reduced gravity.

A one-way coupling strategy was adopted to isolate hydrodynamic displacement effects while circumventing the modeling uncertainties of full heat-transfer and phase-change physics. The transient vapor mass generation rate $\dot{m}_{\rm vapor}(t)$ (boundary-layer plus interface contributions) computed by LIQLEV was exported and prescribed as a time-dependent vapor mass-inlet boundary condition through a small orifice on the bottom wall of the cylindrical domain. This approach reproduces the net volumetric addition arising from wall boundary-layer boil-off without requiring an energy equation or detailed nucleation modeling in the CFD solver.

The representative simulation utilized a $91.4\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.610\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height cylindrical tank at 50\,\% liquid fill, subject to the gravity profile recorded during past NASA GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility experiments. High-resolution prism inflation layers were applied along the tank walls to resolve near-wall liquid climb, and surface tension effects were captured using the continuum surface force (CSF) model. The SST $k$-$\omega$ turbulence model was employed with a fixed time step of $0.001\,\text{s}$ for a $5\,\text{s}$ total simulation.

The LIQLEV-predicted LLR for this configuration at the lowest vent rate ($1.36\,\text{g/s}$ or $0.003\,\text{lbm/s}$) is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_time_history}; the model predicts a smooth rise that reaches its maximum of approximately $81\,\text{mm}$ ($3.2\,\text{in}$) well within the 5-second low-gravity window.

In interpreting drop-tower probe data, the CFD centerline value bounds what an axial probe would measure and the LIQLEV value bounds the worst-case displacement under piston-like assumptions.

\section{Experimental Design and Parameter Sweeps}
\label{sec:exp_design_parameter_sweeps}

The modernized LIQLEV framework was used to identify optimal test conditions for three NASA reduced-gravity facilities. The objective was to ensure that the maximum liquid-vapor interface rise could be fully captured within each facility’s available test duration while preventing lid impingement.

The candidate platforms were:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NASA GRC 2.2-Second Drop Tower:} $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g quality with a highly constrained $2.2\,\text{s}$ test window, requiring rapid interface dynamics to observe the initial transient.
    \item \textbf{NASA GRC 5-Second Zero-Gravity Facility:} $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g quality, with sufficient duration for boundary-layer growth and attainment of the peak LLR.
    \item \textbf{Parabolic Flight Campaigns:} $\sim 17\,\text{s}$ of reduced gravity ($\sim 10^{-2}$ to $10^{-3}\,$g with typical g-jitter), permitting longer-term thermodynamic stabilization and quasi-steady boiling.
\end{itemize}

Facility-specific gravity histories are incorporated through a custom \texttt{gravity\_function} callback. For the 5-second zero-gravity facility, accelerometer data from prior drop-tower tests (normalized time and axial acceleration) are converted to m/s$^2$ (ft/s$^2$ internally). A constant hold value of $\sim 1.4 \times 10^{-3}\,$g is appended beyond the last measured point, allowing each simulation to be integrated past the nominal $5\,\text{s}$ window out to $20\,\text{s}$. This post-cutoff segment is a deliberate \textit{hypothetical} extension---the actual drop tower terminates microgravity at $5\,\text{s}$---used solely to verify that the predicted peak liquid level rise within the real $5\,\text{s}$ window is close to the asymptotic maximum that would be reached given more time. In all time-history figures the shaded region beyond $5\,\text{s}$ marks this extrapolation.

This extension was essential to determine whether the maximum LLR is attained \textit{within} the $5\,\text{s}$ microgravity window or only later. Comparing the time of peak rise against the rigid facility limit identifies optimal tank geometries, fill fractions, and vent rates that fully capture the complete transient---from initial meniscus reorientation through sustained boundary-layer-driven rise---before the end of the test window, while strictly avoiding lid impingement.

\subsection{Governing Parameters}
The parameter sweep systematically quantified the coupled effects of geometry, thermodynamics, venting, and gravity level on transient liquid level rise (LLR). The following variables were varied across the full matrix:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tank Geometries and Aspect Ratios ($L/D$):} The primary grid consisted of 50 diameter values ($61$--$305\,\text{mm}$, or $0.2$--$1.0\,\text{ft}$) and 40 height values ($91$--$762\,\text{mm}$, or $0.3$--$2.5\,\text{ft}$), giving 2000 unique geometries per vent rate and aspect ratios $L/D \approx 2$--$12$. Targeted smaller-scale cases were also evaluated, including Skinner tanks of $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2$--$0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter at fixed $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2\,\text{ft}$) height, plus miniature cylindrical tanks matching Labus et al.\ ($60\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$ height).

    \item \textbf{Gravity Levels:} $10^{-3}\,$g, $10^{-4}\,$g, and $10^{-5}\,$g, bracketing the operational envelopes of all three facilities. Each level was implemented either as a constant or via piecewise \texttt{gravity\_function} callbacks.

    \item \textbf{Initial Fill Levels:} Liquid volume fractions from $20\,\%$ to $80\,\%$. Detailed matrices typically spanned $25$--$65\,\%$ for adequate optical visibility while maintaining margin against lid impingement.

    \item \textbf{Vent Flow Rates:} Constant rates scaled with tank size. For tanks of $61\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2\,\text{ft}$) diameter and larger (including the $0.61\,\text{m}$ Skinner series), four discrete rates between $1.36$ and $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$) were selected. These rates generate measurable LLR while keeping the interface below the tank lid for the chosen fill fractions. Proportionally smaller vent rates were applied to the Labus-scale ($60\,\text{mm}$ $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$) tanks to maintain comparable thermodynamic behavior across scales.
\end{enumerate}

Nitrogen was employed as the baseline fluid for the primary geometric sweeps because it is the planned working fluid for the actual drop-tower experiments. This selection enables rapid computation and provides direct correlation with the planned hardware, while offering excellent optical transparency for high-speed imaging. Separate validation runs using hydrogen were performed against the JAXA $30\,m^3$ LH$_2$ large-scale test data to ensure model fidelity for flight-relevant cryogens.

\subsection{Optimization Criteria}
The optimization process identified tank geometries, initial fill fractions, and vent rates that maximize scientific return within each facility’s rigid time constraint while strictly preventing liquid impingement on the tank lid. Scientific return was defined as the ability to optically capture the complete liquid-vapor interface transient---from rapid meniscus reorientation through sustained boundary-layer-driven rise and eventual stabilization---under high-quality microgravity conditions.

The two primary optimization criteria were:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The peak liquid-vapor interface height must be attained \textit{within} the available reduced-gravity duration and must produce an optically measurable rise of at least $50.8\,\text{mm}$ ($2\,\text{in}$). This minimum threshold ensures the thermodynamic signal exceeds residual g-jitter and imaging-system noise, allowing clean capture of the complete transient with high-speed imaging.
    \item The initial fill fraction must be constrained such that cumulative vapor generation (from both the free interface and wall thermal boundary layer) and the associated volumetric swelling keep the interface below the tank ceiling ($Z_{\rm int}(t) < Z_{\rm tank}$ for all $t \leq t_{\rm facility}$).
\end{itemize}

In microgravity the liquid-vapor interface rapidly transitions from a flat 1$g$ surface to a highly curved meniscus whose equilibrium shape is governed by the contact angle and the minimization of surface energy. Superimposed venting drives upward bulk motion through distributed bubble generation and displacement within the liquid column. 

The Python implementation explicitly tracks the instantaneous interface location $Z_{\rm int}(t)$ (denoted internally as \texttt{h2} or \texttt{zht2}) via the discrete relation
\[
Z_{\rm int}(t) = Z_{\rm int}(t-\Delta t) + \frac{\Delta V_{\rm BL} + \Delta m_{\rm liq}/\rho_{\rm liq}}{A_c}\,\Delta t,
\]
where $\Delta V_{\rm BL}$ is the incremental vapor volume generated in the wall thermal boundary layer, $\Delta m_{\rm liq}$ is the mass of liquid flashed to vapor, $\rho_{\rm liq}$ is the saturated liquid density, and $A_c = \pi D^2/4$ is the tank cross-sectional area. This formulation embodies the one-dimensional piston-like displacement assumption central to the original LIQLEV boundary-layer model.

An early-termination flag halts each simulation the instant $Z_{\rm int} \ge Z_{\rm tank}$, records the overfill condition, and excludes that geometry from further analysis. Liquid level rise values are extracted at multiple strategically chosen snapshots ($t=5$\,s, $t=20$\,s or end-of-run, and the global peak) via linear interpolation. These multi-time results, together with the overfill flag, are exported to structured .xlsx grids. This framework enables automated contour plotting with hatched regions denoting invalid (lid-impingement) configurations, greatly facilitating rapid and informed selection of viable experimental test articles.

\subsection{Influence of Gravity Level on Liquid Level Rise}
Background acceleration exhibits a strong non-linear inverse relationship with maximum LLR. At $10^{-3}\,$g, residual buoyancy forces promote partial vapor bubble stratification and departure, limiting the residence time of vapor within the bulk liquid column. As gravity decreases to $10^{-5}\,$g (representative of the NASA GRC drop towers), bubble departure diameter grows substantially while the relative rise velocity of bubbles approaches zero. Vapor generated in the wall boundary layer and at the interface remains entrained, acting as distributed void volume that inflates the liquid column.

This mechanism is quantified directly in the model through the boundary-layer vapor volume $V_{\rm BL}$ and the interface velocity:
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\left(\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt} + \frac{1}{\rho_l}\frac{dm_l}{dt}\right),
\]
where $A_c = \pi D^2/4$. Consequently, the most aggressive LLR occurs at the lowest gravity levels.

Variable-gravity parabolic profiles ($0.08\,$g or $0.01\,$g low-$g$ plateaus) were also evaluated and confirmed that the full transient can be captured during the low-$g$ portion, yielding rises of $13$--$50\,\text{mm}$ ($0.5$--$2\,\text{in}$) and beyond depending on vent rate and fill. In contrast, the 2.2- and 5-second drop-tower environments, while achieving the same ultra-low gravity quality, do not provide sufficient duration for the interface to reach its asymptotic maximum; higher vent rates are therefore required to generate a measurable rise within the rigid time window.

\subsection{Plateaued vs.\ Sustained-Growth Regimes}
\label{sec:plateaued_vs_sustained}

The transient time histories (Figures~\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories}--\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories_75}) reveal two qualitatively different regimes for the same tank geometry, fluid, and gravity profile, separated only by vent rate. Low-vent-rate configurations approach a clear asymptote within $2$--$3\,\text{s}$, while high-vent-rate configurations exhibit nearly monotonic interface rise that continues through the end of the $20\,\text{s}$ integration. The distinction is intrinsic to the LIQLEV physics under sustained low-gravity venting, not a numerical artifact, and merits explicit discussion because it informs which configurations are admissible test articles.

The interface velocity in LIQLEV is the sum of two source terms:
\[
\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{1}{A_c}\left(\frac{dV_{\rm BL}}{dt} + \frac{1}{\rho_l}\frac{dm_l}{dt}\right).
\]
Both $dV_{\rm BL}/dt$ (boundary-layer vapor generation) and $|dm_l/dt|/\rho_l$ (bulk flashing) are proportional to the instantaneous depressurization rate $|dP/dt|$ through the Clausius--Clapeyron relation. Vapor departure from the boundary layer is governed by the buoyancy term $a_1 \propto \sqrt{g(\rho_l - \rho_v)/\rho_l}$, which scales with the square root of the local gravity level: at $\sim 1.4 \times 10^{-3}\,$g (the held value past $5\,\text{s}$), $a_1$ is reduced by a factor of $\sim$27 relative to its $1\,$g value, severely throttling buoyant bubble departure.

Two competing balances determine whether a configuration plateaus:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Plateaued regime} (low vent rate): the absolute depressurization rate $|dP/dt|$ is small enough that ullage repressurization from boundary-layer evaporation is sufficient to bring the system into a quasi-steady state within the $5\,\text{s}$ window. Vapor generation balances venting, the bulk superheat decays, $V_{\rm BL}$ stabilizes, and $dh/dt \to 0$. For the recommended $91\,\text{mm}$ tank at $1.36$--$2.27\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.005\,\text{lbm/s}$), the value at $t = 5\,\text{s}$ is within $\sim$2\,\% of the asymptotic value (Figure~\ref{fig:liqlev_time_history}).

    \item \textbf{Sustained-growth regime} (high vent rate): $|dP/dt|$ remains large throughout because the tank pressure has not yet decayed to the target final value (e.g., for the $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$), 25\,\% fill case, $P = 45\,\text{kPa}$ at $t = 20\,\text{s}$, still above the $34\,\text{kPa}$ ($5\,\text{psia}$) target). The wall boundary-layer continues producing fresh vapor at a rate that exceeds buoyant departure; in addition, as the interface rises the wetted-wall area $\pi D h(t)$ grows, providing positive feedback that further accelerates $dV_{\rm BL}/dt$. The result is an interface velocity that does not relax to zero during the simulated window: $dh/dt$ is observed to \textit{increase} from $\sim 10^{-2}\,\text{ft/s}$ at $t = 10\,\text{s}$ to $\sim 8 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{ft/s}$ at $t = 20\,\text{s}$ for the same $2.72\,\text{g/s}$, 25\,\% fill case --- an order-of-magnitude acceleration over the second half of the integration. These solutions remain bounded only by the simulation's vent-profile-end termination at $20\,\text{s}$ or by tank fill-up.
\end{itemize}

In physical terms, sustained-growth solutions correspond to drop-tower configurations where the experiment captures only the early transient of an evolving boundary layer rather than a true thermodynamic peak. They are excluded from the recommended test matrix (Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix}) on the grounds that the $5\,\text{s}$ window cannot capture a representative maximum in such cases. This regime distinction also clarifies why the marker convention used in Figures~\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories}--\ref{fig:0p3ft_time_histories_75} is the value at $t = 5\,\text{s}$ (the experimentally measurable peak) rather than the global maximum over the integrated interval: any peak that occurs in the shaded extrapolated region beyond $5\,\text{s}$ is unreachable under the actual facility duty cycle.


\subsection{Tank Aspect Ratio Effects (5-Second Facility)}
A broad parametric sweep was first executed across more than 2000 unique cylindrical geometries (50 diameters from $60\,\text{mm}$ to $305\,\text{mm}$, or $0.197$--$1.0\,\text{ft}$, and 40 heights from $100\,\text{mm}$ to $762\,\text{mm}$, or $0.328$--$2.5\,\text{ft}$) at $10^{-5}\,$g using the gravity profile extracted from prior NASA GRC drop-tower accelerometer data. Beyond the last measured point at $5\,\text{s}$, the acceleration was held constant for realistic extrapolation in the $20\,\text{s}$ snapshot plots. The height-dependent $\epsilon$ formulation was used throughout.

From this matrix, candidate dimensions were down-selected against two practical constraints from historical drop-tower hardware---maximum diameter $\sim 305\,\text{mm}$ ($1\,\text{ft}$) and maximum height $\sim 0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2\,\text{ft}$) to fit inside existing drop capsules---while also encompassing the Labus-scale miniature ($60\,\text{mm}$ $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$).

The primary analysis identified the optimal performance region in the contour plots: the band between the red $50.8\,\text{mm}$ ($2\,\text{in}$) measurable-rise contour and the hatched overfill zones. The selection criterion was to find tanks that produce an optically measurable rise (above the red contour) while remaining below the tank lid (outside the hatched overfill regions) for practical fill fractions and vent rates. Smaller-diameter tanks consistently delivered higher interface velocities because any incremental vapor volume $\Delta V_{\rm vapor}$ produces a larger displacement in the reduced cross-section:
\[
\Delta Z = \frac{\Delta V_{\rm vapor}}{A_c}.
\]

Four representative geometries within this optimal band were examined in detail:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $305\,\text{mm}$ ($1.0\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height, $L/D = 2$
    \item $229\,\text{mm}$ ($0.75\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D \approx 2.67$
    \item $152\,\text{mm}$ ($0.5\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D = 4$
    \item $91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$, $L/D \approx 6.67$
\end{itemize}

Several trends emerged. The $305\,\text{mm}$-diameter tank proved too wide, with excessive margin but insufficient rise within the 5-second window for most conditions. In contrast, the smaller-diameter Skinner-style tanks ($61$--$91\,\text{mm}$) produced much stronger rise signals; however, these narrower tanks often required greater height to prevent impingement, particularly at higher fills. Increasing the fill fraction expanded the usable region, with the best overall balance near $50\,\%$ fill. At $75\,\%$ fill, the hatched overfill regions grew substantially, reducing viable configurations.

The optimal test conditions were therefore identified near $50\,\%$ fill and diameters between $\sim 91\,\text{mm}$ and $\sim 122\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3$--$0.4\,\text{ft}$). Diameters in the $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ range at $0.61\,\text{m}$ height performed particularly well across the selected vent rates, falling between the red measurable-rise contour and the hatched overfill regions.

\subsection{Facility-Specific Performance and Optimal Test Matrix}

\textbf{Parabolic Flight:}
The facility provides approximately $17\,\text{s}$ of reduced gravity, nominally $\sim 10^{-2}\,$g with typical g-jitter. Reference simulations were performed for the existing parabolic-flight test tank of \cite{Opitz2021} (vacuum-jacketed 304L stainless-steel dewar, 100\,L LN$_2$ capacity, $620\,\text{kPa}$ ($90\,\text{psig}$) max pressure). Using conservative acceleration magnitudes ($\sqrt{g_x^2 + g_y^2 + g_z^2}$), the LIQLEV model indicates that rises of $\gtrsim 76\,\text{mm}$ ($3\,\text{in}$) could be achieved during the low-$g$ plateau. However, persistent lateral acceleration components ($10^{-2}$ to $5\times 10^{-3}\,$g) inherent to the parabolic maneuver and attitude corrections excite unidirectional sloshing and asymmetric interfacial modes that contaminate the purely thermodynamic, boundary-layer-driven LLR signal of interest, consistent with prior parabolic-slosh observations in the literature. Parabolic flight was therefore not advanced as a test venue.


\textbf{GRC 2.2-Second Drop Tower:}
The facility operates over a gravity range of $\sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}\,$g. Comprehensive parametric investigations across tank diameters, aspect ratios, initial fill fractions, and vent rates show that the $2.2\,\text{s}$ test window is generally too short for the interface to reach its maximum thermodynamic level rise. Even with high initial fill fractions ($\sim 75$--$80\,\%$) and aggressive vent rates ($0.113$--$0.340\,\text{kg/s}$, or $0.25$--$0.75\,\text{lbm/s}$) intended to drive rapid interface motion within the first $1.5$--$2\,\text{s}$, only the early transient phase of boundary-layer vapor generation and liquid displacement can be captured; sustained boiling does not have time to develop the peak rise.

In comparison, the GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility provides both a longer test duration and a lower residual gravity level ($\sim 10^{-5}\,$g), enabling substantially higher measurable peak rise once the configuration is optimized. Achieving a measurable peak rise in the 2.2-second facility would require even narrower tanks and substantially higher vent rates than those viable in the 5-second facility---geometries that no longer fit within the existing drop-capsule envelope.

\textbf{GRC 5-Second Zero-Gravity Facility:}
The facility operates at $\sim 10^{-5}\,$g. Comprehensive parametric sweeps encompassing thousands of geometries (diameters $61$--$305\,\text{mm}$, heights $91$--$762\,\text{mm}$), initial fill fractions, vent rates, and $\epsilon$ schedules identified this platform as the optimal test venue. It provides the ideal balance of true high-quality microgravity and sufficient test duration for the wall boundary-layer vapor generation and resulting liquid displacement to develop fully, enabling the interface to attain its maximum thermodynamic rise---unlike the severely time-constrained 2.2-second facility. The low residual gravity further enhances measurable displacement by minimizing buoyant bubble departure and increasing vapor residence time within the bulk liquid column, producing substantially larger rises than achievable under the higher-$g$ environment typical of parabolic flight.

Recommended configurations use a small-diameter cylindrical tank ($91\,\text{mm}$ ($0.3\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height, $L/D \approx 6.67$) with initial fill fractions of $50$--$75\,\%$ and constant vent rates in the $1.36$--$2.72\,\text{g/s}$ ($0.003$--$0.006\,\text{lbm/s}$) range. Under these conditions, the LIQLEV solver predicts peak rises between $\sim 60$ and $\sim 170\,\text{mm}$ ($\sim 2.4$ and $\sim 6.7\,\text{in}$) attained between $3.5$ and $4.8\,\text{s}$, well within the available $5\,\text{s}$ window and without lid impingement.

The proposed experimental test matrix is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix}. Twelve unique conditions span two fill levels ($50\,\%$ and $75\,\%$) and three vent-rate categories (Low / Med / High) at both $1\,$g (ground baselines) and $10^{-5}\,$g (drop-tower microgravity), all with liquid nitrogen (LN$_2$) and gaseous nitrogen (GN$_2$). Each test will be performed twice for repeatability assessment, yielding approximately 15 drops total once shake-down repeats are excluded.

Vent rates in the test matrix are intentionally specified as qualitative \emph{Low / Med / High} categories rather than the LIQLEV-predicted numerical values ($1.36$, $2.04$, $2.72\,\text{g/s}$ as candidate centerlines for the three bins). Whether the test apparatus can reliably deliver these specific flow rates---and hold them stable across the $5\,\text{s}$ drop window---is itself a hardware-validation question that must be answered by the $1\,$g shake-down tests (Tests~1--6) before the microgravity drops (Tests~7--12). Tests~1--6 therefore serve a dual purpose: they calibrate the Low / Med / High categories to numerical mass-flow rates for the actual hardware, and they provide $1\,$g reference data for direct comparison against the matched-condition microgravity drops. Final mapping of Low / Med / High to specific mass-flow rates will be reported alongside the experimental data.

\subsection{Effect of Epsilon (\texorpdfstring{$\epsilon$}{epsilon}) and Model Sensitivity}
The boil-off partitioning parameter $\epsilon$ represents the instantaneous fraction of total vapor mass generated within the wall thermal boundary layer relative to the vapor generated at the liquid-vapor free surface. In the modernized Python implementation of LIQLEV, $\epsilon$ is evaluated at every solver time step. When operating in the default height-dependent mode (neps = 0), it is computed directly from the geometric wetted-area ratio according to the Moran formulation:

\[
\epsilon(t) = \frac{\pi D \, h(t)}{\pi D \, h(t) + \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4}},
\]

where $D$ is the tank diameter and $h(t)$ is the instantaneous liquid height. This expression assumes that the rate of vapor production scales proportionally with the respective heat-transfer areas (sidewall wetted surface versus free interface).

A sensitivity study quantified the dominant influence of $\epsilon$ at low gravity levels. The time-dependent $\epsilon(t)$ schedule derived from the evolving wetted-area ratio produces physically representative LLR behavior. When this dynamic schedule was compared against constant-$\epsilon$ cases ($\epsilon = 0.4$, $0.625$, and $0.8$) under identical geometry, fill, and vent rate, peak LLR differed by up to $30$--$40\,\%$. The effect is most pronounced at $10^{-5}\,$g, where reduced buoyancy allows wall-generated vapor bubbles to remain entrained longer, increasing the volumetric displacement of the liquid column. These differences and the temporal evolution of $\epsilon(t)$ itself are illustrated in the epsilon-sensitivity time-history and contour plots compiled for each reduced-gravity facility.

\subsection{Effect of Tank Size on Meniscus Reorientation Time}

Additional 3D VOF CFD simulations were performed with the same modeling framework (continuum surface-force model, near-wall prism layers, SST $k$-$\omega$ turbulence) to assess the effect of tank scale on liquid--vapor interface reorientation. Two geometries of direct relevance to the experimental matrix were examined: the Skinner-scale tank ($61\,\text{mm}$ ($0.2\,\text{ft}$) diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ ($2.0\,\text{ft}$) height) and the Labus-scale tank ($60\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $100\,\text{mm}$ height). Each geometry was simulated at three gravity levels ($10^{-3}\,$g, $10^{-4}\,$g, $10^{-5}\,$g) to quantify the time required for the interface to reorient from a flat 1\,$g$ surface to the equilibrium curved meniscus.

The results (Figures~\ref{fig:cfd_meniscus_0p2ft} and \ref{fig:cfd_meniscus_6cm}) reveal two trends. First, reorientation time increases markedly with decreasing gravity. Second, the smaller Labus-scale tank reaches a stable meniscus significantly faster than the $61\,\text{mm}$ tank at any given gravity, consistent with the reduced Bond number (gravitational-to-surface-tension ratio) in smaller geometries and lower gravity, where surface tension dominates more rapidly.

The CFD-derived meniscus formation times provide important quantitative insight into the pre-venting interface dynamics. For the recommended $61$--$91\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ height tanks, the simulations indicate that complete reorientation may require more time than is available before venting begins in the GRC 5-second facility. Whether full pre-venting meniscus stabilization is required to meet the scientific objectives remains an open question to be resolved by the forthcoming physical experiments. Nevertheless, the results confirm that the selected tank scale remains well suited to capture the targeted boundary-layer-driven LLR once venting commences.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\paragraph{Findings.} The legacy LIQLEV boundary-layer model has been reimplemented as a parallelized, CoolProp-integrated Python framework with a Numba JIT-compiled solver that delivers $\sim$40$\times$ speedup over the original pure-Python loop while reproducing the reference solution to better than 1 part in $10^6$. The modernized model has been validated against the JAXA $30\,\text{m}^3$ LH$_2$ depressurization experiment, where a constant $\epsilon$ in the range $75$--$100$ (\texttt{bulk\_fake} mode) reproduces the experimentally observed $174\,\text{mm}$ ($6.85\,\text{in}$) interface rise within the first few seconds, with $\epsilon \approx 100$ providing the closest match. Three-dimensional VOF CFD cross-validation confirms that LIQLEV provides a conservatively high estimate of bulk liquid displacement that matches the volume-equivalent flat-interface height to within centimeters at the $91\,\text{mm} \times 0.61\,\text{m}$ scale. Parametric sweeps across more than 2000 cylindrical geometries identify the GRC 5-second zero-gravity facility as the optimal test venue, with a recommended test article of $91\,\text{mm}$ diameter $\times$ $0.61\,\text{m}$ height ($L/D \approx 6.67$), nitrogen working fluid, and a 12-condition test matrix spanning $50\,\%$ and $75\,\%$ initial fill at three vent-rate categories (Low / Med / High, calibrated to LIQLEV-predicted target rates of $1.36$--$2.72\,\text{g/s}$ in the $1\,$g shake-down phase). Under these conditions the LIQLEV-predicted peak rise is attained at $3.5$--$4.8\,\text{s}$, comfortably within the facility's microgravity window.

\paragraph{Forward Plan.} The FY26+ drop-tower campaign will execute the matrix in Table~\ref{tab:recommended_test_matrix} using high-speed imaging, sidewall temperature probes, and calibrated ullage pressure transducers. The data will close the validation loop on the modernized model, drive the development of an explicit bulk-liquid boiling source term to retire the \texttt{bulk\_fake} engineering approximation, and establish the first sub-meter-scale, flight-relevant transient LLR data set for cryogenic fluid management correlation development. The 2.2-second drop tower is reserved for early-transient depressurization studies; parabolic flight is not pursued due to lateral g-jitter contamination of the boundary-layer-driven signal.

\paragraph{Programmatic Impact.} The validated modeling framework and pre-test design directly support vent-system sizing for HLS-class vehicles, in-space cryogenic depots, and Mars transfer stages, all of which are critically dependent on reliable microgravity vent operations to meet propellant residual and $\Delta v$ margin requirements. By replacing single-data-point reliance on the 1966 AS-203 flight with a sub-meter-scale ground experiment anchored by a verified, fast, and extensible analytical tool, the proposed campaign reduces a long-standing technical risk on the path from current cryogenic fluid management practice to long-duration human spaceflight. The Python implementation, including \texttt{LIQLEV-Python-mp} and the JIT-accelerated solver, is available from the authors upon request.
